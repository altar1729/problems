Some Problems I am interested in

Here is a list of problems which I am really interested in. This is not a comprehensive list but just a sample.

Union-Closed Sets Conjecture

This is probably a conjecture that lies in the heart of most combinatorialists. It was conjectured by Frankl in 1970s. Suppose we have a family of sets $\mathcal{F}$ on the ground set $[n]$ which is closed under the operation of union, i.e. if $A,B\in \mathcal{F}$, then $A\cup B\in \mathcal{F}$. Then, there exists an element $i\in [n]$ which is contained in at least $\frac{|\mathcal{F}|}{2}$ sets. There has been a recent development in this conjecture by Justin Gilmer , which used very clever entropy technique to prove that there exists an element $i \in[n]$ which is contained in at least $\frac{3-\sqrt{5}}{2}|\mathcal{F}|$ many sets.

Distinct Subset Sums

This is one of my favourite problems. It was conjectured by Erdős in 1930s and is number 1 in the Erdős Problems website . Let $a_1\lt a_2 \lt a_3 \lt \ldots \lt a_n$ be distinct positive integers where all subset sums are distinct. Then, $a_n \geq c2^{n}$ for some constant $c>0$.

Induced Saturation for Posets

We call the family of sets $\{S_1,\ldots,S_k\}=\mathcal{S}\subset 2^{[n]}$, induced saturated for $\mathcal{P}$

  • if there exists no induced copy of $\mathcal{P}$ in $\mathcal{S}$, and
  • if for any $A\in 2^{[n]}\setminus \mathcal{S}$, $\mathcal{S}\cup \{A\}$ contains an induced copy of $\mathcal{P}$.
The minimum number of sets in an induced saturated family is called the induced saturation number for $\mathcal{P}$. Denote this by $sat^*(n,\mathcal{P})$. There are a lot of open problems in this area. The following two are my favourites:
  • Let $\Diamond$ be the diamond poset with four elements, one unique minimal, one unique maximal and two incomparable elements. Does the induced saturation number for the diamond poset $\geq n+1$, $sat^*(n,\Diamond)\geq n+1$?
  • Given any poset $\mathcal{P}$, do we have a linear upper bound in the dimension $n$ for the induced saturation number, $sat^*(n,\mathcal{P})=O(n)$?

Maker-Breaker on $\mathbb{Z}^2$

Consider two players, one maker and the other breaker, playing the following game turn by turn. Maker starts by switching an edge on the underlying graph $\mathbb{Z}^2$. In the next turn, Breaker deletes one edge of the underlying graph permanently. Maker's aim is to construct an infinite connected component and Breaker's aim is to prevent Maker from achieving this. In this version, it is known that Maker has a strategy to win the game. However, there are many open variants of this game. The following two are intriguing:

  • Suppose Maker switches on $a$ edges at one turn, and Breaker deletes $b$ edges permanently. For which $(a,b)$, Maker has a winning strategy and for which $(a,b)$, Breaker has a winning strategy. Even the simplest $(2,2)$ version is not known.
  • Suppose, we work with a randomly sparsed version of $\mathbb{Z}^2$, i.e. before starting the game, delete each edge with probability $1-p$ independently of each other. Then start the game on this graph. What can be said in this case? An examplary paper is this paper written by V. Dvorák, A. Mond, V. Souza .

Sendov's Conjecture

Suppose a complex polynomial $f$ of degree $n\geq 2$ has all of its zeroes in the unit disk $\{z: |z|<1\}$, then for each such zero $z_0$, there is a zero of the derivative $f'$ in the closed unit disk $\{z: |z-z_0|<1 \}$. There has been a lot of improvements concerning this problem. It was known for $n\leq 9$ long before and in the last decade, Terry Tao has proved it for $n$ sufficiently large. However, his method of compactness does not provide any quantitative bounds on how large $n$ should be. This is a dream project which is too hard, but I am interested in finding quantitative bounds, which would need completely new methods.

Simplexity of the Hypercube

Consider the $n$-dimensional cube, $[0,1]^n$. Let $S$ be a family of $n$-dimensional simplices. $S$ is called a triangulation of the cube if, for all $\Delta \in S$, the vertices of $\Delta$ are a subset of the vertices of the cube: $\{0,1\}^n$, and $\cup_{\Delta\in S}\Delta=[0,1]^n$, and distinct simplices are disjoint only meeting at faces. We denote by $tri(n)$, the minimal number of simplices which makes up a triangulation of the cube. What is the right asymptotics for $tri(n)$? This has some known lower bounds, which has been established using bounds on the maximum determinant of $0-1$ matrices.
